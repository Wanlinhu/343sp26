# Live Coding: A User’s Manual (pp. 1–8) — Reading Reflection

## Two things that were new or surprising to me

1) **Live coding resists fixed definitions and even resists being “owned.”**  
I was surprised by the idea that *having no formal definition* (or only a definition that allows for its own redefinition) creates resistance to hierarchical control—so live coding can’t easily be “owned” by established practitioners or institutions. This made me think of live coding as a living practice that stays open and evolves through use, instead of becoming a closed tradition.

2) **“Invisibility” as a Big Tech goal—and why that’s a problem (especially in the AI hype era).**  
I was inspired by the argument that Big Tech wants computers to become invisible so using them feels “natural,” which can make the systems shaping our lives harder to notice. This feels especially relevant now, when AI tools are everywhere and it’s easy to accept them as normal without questioning what’s happening underneath. Live coding’s emphasis on s howing process feels like a strong counter-move.

## Two things that were not new or didn’t surprise me

1) **Users as both exploited and capable of reinvention.**  
The idea that users can be subtly exploited by systems, but can also act as a force for reinvention, did not surprise me. This dual role of the user already feels familiar in many discussions around technology, platforms, and digital power structures.

2) **The mutual adaptation between humans and live code.**  
It didn’t surprise me that when we write code live, we adapt it to our needs, and it adapts us in return. The idea that live coding involves bodies, communication, and action aligns with my existing understanding of live performance and human–computer interaction.
